Four Families · Five Centuries · Three Countries

Our Family
Heritage

The story of Gordon & Mary Gemeny — and the four lineages that converge in their children and grandchildren.

Gemeny · Gordon/DuBois · Sanderson · Burgess

Starting from Gordon & Mary Gemeny, this archive traces four family lines back through centuries — from French Huguenots founding New Paltz to English knights in Tudor Warwickshire, from a sea captain lost at sea to Irish immigrants in Washington, from North Carolina farmers to Maryland craftsmen. 165 ancestors. 520 years. Three countries.

Key Figures
Generation 0 · Colonial

Anthony Gemini

c. 1730

Sailed to North America c. 1755. Married Katherine Richards in Salem. The founding ancestor of every Gemeny in America.
Generation 0 · Colonial

Katherine Richards

c. 1730

Wife of Anthony Gemini. Married October 17, 1756 in Salem, MA. Later married Ebenezer Aborn.
Generation 1 · Early Republic

John “The Sea Captain”

1769 – ?

Ship's master who vanished at sea. His son was hidden in a cupboard to keep him from sailing on the final voyage.
Generation 1 · Early Republic

Naomi Tewksbury

c. 1770

Changed the family surname from Gemini to Gemeny. Operated a rooming house in Baltimore after John's disappearance.
Generation 2 · Antebellum

John Gemeny II

c. 1791 – c. 1870

Built Pleasant View in Kinsale, VA. Father of nine children. Every living Gemeny descends from him and Matilda Figg.
Generation 3 · Civil War

Benjamin Gemeny

1835 – ?

Imprisoned as a Union spy in Confederate Virginia during the Civil War. Subject of Mary's memoir.
Generation 3 · Civil War

Mary E. Roberts

1836 – 1937

Wrote “A Reminiscence of the War of the Rebellion” in 1913. Lived to be approximately 101 years old.
Generation 5 · Interwar

Andrew “Pop” Gemeny

1896 – 1989

Recorded oral histories in the 1970s preserving firsthand accounts of early 20th century family life.
Generation 6 · Postwar

W. Gordon Gemeny

1930 – 2017

Authored the Thomas Gemini origin essay in 2002, tracing possible family roots to a 1540s engraver in England.
